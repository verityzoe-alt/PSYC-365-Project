Fine-Tuning the Details:
Post-encoding Music Differentially Impacts General and Detailed Memory

Kayla R. Clark and Stephanie L. Leal

I. PREVIOUS RESEARCH

  • 1) Music has been suggested to enhance memory consolidation by inducing emotional arousal, known as the emotional arousal hypothesis.

  • 2) Features that influence music-induced emotional arousal include emotional valence, familiarity, and pleasure derived from listening.

  • 3) In emotional memory, there is a tradeoff between gist-based and detailed information, in accordance with the Yerkes-Dodson law.

  • 4) In studying episodic memory, mnemonic discrimination tasks are often used to isolate detail-based pattern separation from gist-based pattern completion.

Big picture question:

How does post-encoding music influence general versus detailed memory?

III. EXPERIMENTAL DESIGN

Click a milestone to see details.

Participant arrives

130 Rice University Students, aged 18-35, arrive for the session and begin the study procedure.

Encoding phase

Participants viewed 128 images of common household objects and classified them as "indoor" or "outdoor" to ensure incidental learning, this is the inital learning task.

Self-assessment of emotion #1

Immediately after learning, participants report their current affective state before the intervention.

Self-assessment of emotion #2

Participants complete a second self-assessment to capture change in affect after the intervention.

Music intervention (10 min)

While completing the first self-assessment survey, they listened to one of six acoustic conditions (see IVs below) for 10 minutes. After 10 min, participants continued completing surveys in silence for an additional 20 min to allow for emotional arousal to dissipate before the retrieval phase.

Surveys (30 min)

Participants continued surveys in silence to allow arousal to dissipate.

Retrieval phase

Participants performed a Mnemonic Discrimination Task (MDT), a memory assesment, where they viewed 192 images (some identical to the first phase “targets”, some similar "lures," and some entirely new "foils") and identified them as "Old" or "New".

Music-rating task

Participants completed a task where they provided subjective ratings for the specific music or sounds they had heard during the intervention. They evaluated these stimuli across four key dimensions: emotional arousal, emotional valence, familiarity, and pleasantness.

Surveys

To conclude the session, participants completed final questionnaires that assessed their musical background, specifically focusing on their musical expertise and how they typically engage with music in their daily lives.

Independent variables (IVs)

6 auditory conditions:

  • 4 high-arousal music conditions, stratified by emotional valence and familiarity.
  • 1 neutral non-music condition.
  • 1 silent condition.

Dependent variables (DVs)

  • target recognition: measure of general memory for repeated images.
  • lure discrimination: measure of detailed memory to discriminate between "old" and "new" images.
  • emotional arousal: measured by the use of a 9x9 affect grid.

IV. RESULTS

R1: memory vs. emotional arousal

There were individual differences in music-induced emotional arousal; not all participants increased in arousal from listening to music, with some showing decreases instead.

R2: emotional arousal vs. memory

There were no significant differences in memory across experimental groups for either target recognition or lure discrimination.

However, to account for the individual differences in arousal, participants were clustered based on their emotional arousal score change from baseline to post-treatment.
